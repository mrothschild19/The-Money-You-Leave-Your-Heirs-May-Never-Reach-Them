Three Courts, Three Years, One Question: Can Courts Ignore the Law?

Pennsylvania Supreme Court Asked to Decide Whether Orphans' Court Orders Issued Without Jurisdiction Are Void—Or Whether Families Must Live With "Legal" Theft

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — When a Delaware County Orphans' Court judge signed orders transferring control of the Roth family trust to a self-nominated "proposed trustee," the beneficiaries who were never notified faced a choice: accept that a stranger now controlled their inheritance, or fight back through a court system that had already failed them.

They chose to fight. Three years later, their case has climbed from Delaware County Orphans' Court to the Superior Court of Pennsylvania to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court—where a Petition for Allowance of Appeal now presents a fundamental question about the rule of law: Are court orders issued without jurisdiction void from the start, or must families accept them because a judge signed them?

THE LEGAL JOURNEY

  • 2023: Delaware County Orphans' Court issues orders appointing successor trustee—without notice to 9 of 10 beneficiaries
  • 2024: Appeal filed to Superior Court of Pennsylvania (No. 1409 EDA 2024)
  • April 1, 2026: Superior Court affirms Orphans' Court orders
  • April 15, 2026: Reargument denied; Motion to Vacate filed in Orphans' Court
  • May 2026: Petition for Allowance of Appeal filed with Pennsylvania Supreme Court

The legal argument is straightforward: Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure 2227(b) requires that indispensable parties be joined in any action affecting their interests. The failure to join indispensable parties is not a mere procedural technicality—it deprives the court of jurisdiction. And orders issued without jurisdiction are void ab initio, meaning void from the start.

But getting courts to acknowledge that they lacked jurisdiction when they signed orders is another matter entirely.

"The Superior Court acknowledged that not all beneficiaries received notice. But rather than finding that the Orphans' Court lacked jurisdiction, the court held that the appellant 'waived' the issue by not raising it sooner. How can you waive an objection to proceedings you were never told about? That's like saying you waived your right to attend a meeting you were never invited to."
— Legal analysis from appeal brief

THE CATCH-22: The beneficiaries who were never notified couldn't object to proceedings they didn't know about. Now, courts are saying they should have objected earlier. It's a legal circle that leaves families with no recourse.

THE STAKES: If the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declines to hear the case, or rules against the beneficiaries, it will send a message that court orders trump due process—that once a judge signs, even without jurisdiction, families must accept the result. But if the Court grants review and finds the orders void, it could set a precedent protecting families across the Commonwealth.

THE BROADER IMPLICATION: This isn't just about one trust. Every trust in Pennsylvania could be vulnerable to the same hijacking scheme. If courts won't enforce joinder requirements, then the rule of law means nothing—and trust predators have a roadmap.

A Test for Pennsylvania's New Elder Exploitation Unit: Will the Attorney General Investigate "Legal" Trust Theft?

In 2025, Pennsylvania Created a Dedicated Elder Exploitation Section. A Delaware County Family's Case Could Be Its First Major Test.

HARRISBURG, PA — In 2025, Pennsylvania Attorney General Michelle A. Henry announced the creation of a dedicated Elder Exploitation Section within the Office of Attorney General—a response to the growing crisis of financial abuse targeting older Pennsylvanians. The announcement highlighted that elder financial exploitation is now a $36.5 billion annual problem nationwide.

But the Roth family case raises a question the new unit may not have anticipated: What happens when the exploitation happens through the court system itself?

THE COMPLAINT: A formal complaint has been filed with the Pennsylvania Attorney General Elder Exploitation Section alleging that a Delaware County attorney exploited Pennsylvania's Orphans' Court procedures to gain control of a family trust—without notice to 9 of 10 beneficiaries, without verification of his relationship to the trust, and without a hearing.

THE UNIQUE CHALLENGE: Most elder exploitation cases involve scams, fraud, or undue influence outside the legal system. The Roth case is different. The exploitation happened through valid court orders signed by a judge. The "predator" didn't break the law—he used the law.

This creates a jurisdictional puzzle for law enforcement:

  • If court orders were signed by a judge, is it exploitation or just aggressive litigation?
  • Can the Attorney General investigate attorneys who used court procedures to gain control of assets?
  • Does "legal" mean "unprosecutable" even when the result is financial exploitation?

THE ATTORNEY GENERAL'S MANDATE: The Elder Exploitation Section was created to investigate and prosecute financial crimes against older Pennsylvanians. The Roth family complaint argues that the unit's jurisdiction should extend to exploitation facilitated by court proceedings—because the victims are the same, the financial harm is the same, and the perpetrators are simply more sophisticated.

"My parents were elderly when this scheme was set in motion. They thought their trust would protect their children. Instead, the courts became the weapon used against our family. If the Elder Exploitation Section only investigates scams and fraud, then it's missing the most insidious form of exploitation—the kind that comes with a judge's signature."
— Miriam Roth Mandel

WHAT'S AT STAKE: If the Attorney General declines to investigate cases where exploitation occurred through court proceedings, it creates a roadmap for sophisticated predators: use the courts, get valid orders, and you're immune from prosecution. But if the unit takes on the case, it could establish that "legal" exploitation is still exploitation—and that Pennsylvania will protect its elderly citizens even when the weapon is the court system itself.

THE COMPLAINT STATUS: The formal complaint to the Elder Exploitation Section is pending review. The family awaits word on whether the Attorney General will investigate.

$124 Trillion at Risk: The Great Wealth Transfer Meets the Trust Hijackers

Over the Next 25 Years, the Largest Transfer of Wealth in History Will Pass to Baby Boomers' Heirs—But Pennsylvania's Court System May Let Predators Intercept It

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — Financial experts call it the "Great Wealth Transfer." Over the next 25 years, an estimated $124 trillion in assets will pass from the Silent Generation and Baby Boomers to their children and grandchildren. It is the largest intergenerational transfer of wealth in human history.

But here's what the experts aren't talking about: A significant portion of that wealth is held in trusts, and Pennsylvania's Orphans' Court system has a vulnerability that could let predators intercept it before it ever reaches the intended beneficiaries.

$124 TRILLION

The estimated wealth transferring to heirs over the next 25 years

THE SCALE OF THE PROBLEM: Pennsylvania has over 12 million residents, with approximately 2.5 million over age 65. Many of these families have established trusts to protect and transfer their wealth. But the Roth family case demonstrates that those trusts may be vulnerable to hijacking by anyone willing to file a petition in Orphans' Court.

THE MATH IS STARK:

  • If just 1% of Pennsylvania's elderly population holds trusts vulnerable to hijacking, that's 25,000 trusts at risk
  • If the average trust value is $500,000, that's $12.5 billion in assets that could be intercepted
  • If predators succeed in just 1% of cases, that's $125 million stolen from Pennsylvania families—through "legal" court proceedings

THE PERFECT STORM: Several factors converge to make this the ideal environment for trust hijacking:

  • Aging population: More elderly Pennsylvanians means more trusts—and more opportunities for exploitation
  • Complex families: Blended families, estranged relatives, and geographic dispersion make it easier for predators to operate without detection
  • Digital records: Court filings are now online, making it easier for predators to identify target trusts
  • Under-resourced courts: Orphans' Court judges handle heavy caseloads with limited time for scrutiny
  • Minimal oversight: Once a successor trustee is appointed, there's little ongoing supervision
"My parents worked their entire lives to build what they would leave to their children. They paid attorneys to create a trust. They thought they had done everything right. They never imagined that the weakest link in the chain was the court system itself. How many other Pennsylvania families are sitting on trusts that could be hijacked tomorrow?"
— Miriam Roth Mandel

THE POLICY QUESTION: As Pennsylvania prepares for the Great Wealth Transfer, will the Commonwealth strengthen protections for family trusts—or will it remain a state where predators can exploit court procedures to intercept inheritances?

THE REFORM AGENDA: The Roth family case highlights specific legislative fixes that could protect the $124 trillion wealth transfer:

  1. Mandatory notice to all beneficiaries before successor trustee appointments
  2. Verification requirements for petitioners' relationship to trusts
  3. Hearing requirements for contested or unverified appointments
  4. Enhanced oversight of successor trustees
  5. Attorney General authority to investigate court-facilitated exploitation

THE BOTTOM LINE: The Great Wealth Transfer is happening with or without protections. Pennsylvania has a choice: protect families now, or spend the next 25 years watching inheritances disappear into the hands of trust hijackers.
