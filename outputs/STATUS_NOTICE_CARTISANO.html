II. RULE 1701 DOES NOT DEPRIVE THIS COURT OF AUTHORITY TO VACATE VOID ORDERS

Respondents may argue that Pa.R.A.P. 1701(a) — which provides that after an appeal is taken, the trial court "may no longer proceed further in the matter" — divests this Court of authority to act on the Motion to Vacate while the matter is pending in the appellate courts. That argument fails for a fundamental reason: Rule 1701 presupposes the existence of a valid order that has been appealed. It cannot operate to preserve jurisdiction that this Court never possessed.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court has held without qualification that the absence of an indispensable party "deprives the court of jurisdiction" — not that it creates a defect that may later be cured, but that the court is without power to act from the outset. Tigue v. Basalyga, 451 Pa. 436, 438, 304 A.2d 119, 120 (1973); Sprague v. Casey, 520 Pa. 38, 48, 550 A.2d 184 (1988). Orders entered without subject matter jurisdiction are not merely voidable — they are void ab initio, without legal existence from the moment of their entry. In re Navarra, 185 A.3d 342, 349 (Pa. Super. 2018) ("The absence of an indispensable party renders any order or decree of the court null and void."). A void order is not an "order" in any legally cognizable sense. Rule 1701 has no subject matter to operate upon when the underlying orders are nullities.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court confirmed this principle in Glen-Gery Corp. v. Zoning Hearing Board of Dover Township, 589 Pa. 135, 907 A.2d 1033 (2006), holding that an act void ab initio "never became effective" and therefore no appellate time limits — and by extension no appellate procedural rules — are triggered by its purported entry. The same logic applies here: if the orders entered in this proceeding are void ab initio, then Rule 1701's appellate-stay provision was never triggered, because there was nothing to appeal and nothing to stay.

Similarly, the fraud-on-the-court doctrine independently places this matter beyond Rule 1701's reach. Hazel-Atlas Glass Co. v. Hartford-Empire Co., 322 U.S. 238, 246 (1944), holds that courts retain inherent power to address fraud on the court "without regard to the passage of time or the finality of the underlying judgment." The word "finality" in Hazel-Atlas encompasses precisely the argument that an appellate affirmance — however characterized — cures the fraud. It does not. A court's inherent power to address fraud on the court is not suspended by appellate proceedings. It exists independently of them.

IV. REQUEST FOR IMMEDIATE RULING

Petitioner respectfully requests that this Court rule on the Motion to Vacate at the earliest practicable date.

This Court's authority to vacate void orders is not suspended by Rule 1701 or by the pendency of appellate proceedings. A court cannot lose jurisdiction it never had. Glen-Gery Corp. v. Zoning Hearing Board of Dover Township, 589 Pa. 135, 150, 907 A.2d 1033, 1042 (2006). Orders entered without subject matter jurisdiction are void ab initio — legal nullities from the moment of entry — and may be vacated by this Court now, without awaiting action by the Superior Court or the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania.

Every month that these void orders remain in force is another month that approximately $756,000 in trust assets — funds created specifically to provide medical care for a profoundly disabled beneficiary, Lee Benson Roth — remain frozen behind orders that have no legal effect. The harm is concrete, ongoing, and entirely preventable. There is no procedural obstacle to this Court acting immediately.

The pendency of the Petition for Allowance of Appeal in the Supreme Court does not counsel delay. The Motion to Vacate and the Petition for Allowance of Appeal are complementary, not sequential: the Motion to Vacate asks this Court to recognize, from the ground up, that it lacked subject matter jurisdiction from the inception of these proceedings; the Petition for Allowance of Appeal asks the Supreme Court to provide binding statewide guidance on the legal principles that require that recognition. This Court need not — and should not — wait for the Supreme Court to act before vindicating its own jurisdictional integrity.
