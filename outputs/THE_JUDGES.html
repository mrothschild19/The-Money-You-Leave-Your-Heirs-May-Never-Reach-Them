The Mandatory Recusal Framework

Pennsylvania law creates a mandatory three-step recusal process:

  1. Step 1: The Duty to Recuse — Rule 2.11(A) of the PA Code of Judicial Conduct mandates disqualification when impartiality might reasonably be questioned. The word "shall" makes this mandatory.
  2. Step 2: Referral to Another Judge — Commonwealth v. Dip holds that a judge who is the subject of a recusal motion cannot adjudicate that motion.
  3. Step 3: Reassignment Authority — 42 Pa.C.S. § 322(a) and Pa.R.J.A. 703 authorize the President-Judge to reassign cases.

Critical Discovery: New evidence demonstrates that Defendant's Notice of Intent to file a Judgment of Non-Pros was premature and violates Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure § 237.1(a).

Timeline of Events

July 17, 2025 Plaintiff filed the Complaint
September 15, 2025 Deadline for filing Certificate of Merit (COM)
August 25, 2025 Notice of Intent mailed BEFORE the COM deadline
November 21, 2025 Discovery that Notice was premature

Legal Basis - Why the Notice Was Invalid

"No judgment of default shall be entered by the prothonotary unless the praecipe for entry includes a certification that a written notice of intention to file the praecipe was mailed... at least ten (10) days prior to the date of the filing of the praecipe." — Pa.R.C.P. § 237.1(a)

Key Legal Principles:

  • The notice may be sent only AFTER the default has occurred
  • Bartolomeo v. Marshall (69 A.3d 610, Pa. Sup. Ct. 2013) — the 10-day notice must follow a prior failure to answer
  • Gennaro Asphalt Inc. v. Zarlengo Bros. — a notice sent before the order it relates to is ineffective
Because the COM was not due until September 15, the plaintiff was NOT in default on August 25. Consequently, the Notice of Intent was PREMATURE and the judgment is voidable.

  2. Vacate the Judgment of Non-Pros entered on August 25, 2025
  3. Reassign the case to a neutral judge
  4. Award treble damages under the Dragonetti Act and elder-abuse damages
