## THREAD 2: THE LEGAL BREAKDOWN
*For legal audience and those wanting deeper analysis*

---

**TWEET 1:**

🧵 LEGAL THREAD: What happens when a court enters orders without jurisdiction?

A deep dive into the legal framework behind the Roth Trust case—and why "void ab initio" matters.

#EstateLaw #TrustLaw #Pennsylvania

---

**TWEET 2:**

Start with the basics: In Pennsylvania, Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b) requires joinder of "indispensable parties."

Who's indispensable? Anyone whose interests are so directly connected that a final decree cannot be made without affecting them.

Trust beneficiaries = indispensable parties.

---

**TWEET 3:**

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court in *Sprague v. Casey* (1988):

"The validity of an order is... always subject to challenge" when the court lacks jurisdiction.

Not 30 days. Not one year. ALWAYS.

---

**TWEET 4:**

20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(b) specifically requires that "all qualified beneficiaries" be given notice before a successor trustee is appointed.

Not some beneficiaries. Not the ones the petitioner likes. ALL qualified beneficiaries.

---

**TWEET 5:**

The trial court proceeded with only 1 of 10 beneficiaries served.

Nine beneficiaries—including adult children and grandchildren—learned about the proceedings only after orders had already been entered.

---

**TWEET 6:**

The Superior Court's affirmance on April 1, 2026 raises a disturbing question:

Can an appellate court affirm orders that are void on their face?

The answer should be no. Void orders cannot be "affirmed"—they simply don't exist.

---

**TWEET 7:**

Key precedent: *McEvoy v. Quaker City Cab Co.*, 267 Pa. 527 (1920)

"A decree void on its face may be attacked anywhere... directly or collaterally, by parties or strangers."

Void = nonexistent.

---

**TWEET 8:**

The due process violation is clear under *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank* (U.S. Supreme Court):

Notice must be "reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties."

Zero notice to 90% of beneficiaries = zero due process.

---

**TWEET 9:**

The pending Motion to Vacate in Orphans' Court argues that because the court never had jurisdiction, its orders are void and must be vacated.

Not discretionary. Mandatory.

Void orders have no legal effect.

---

**TWEET 10:**

For trust and estate practitioners: This case should concern you.

If courts can proceed without required parties, what protections do beneficiaries actually have?

The answer may be: far fewer than you think.

---

**TWEET 11 (Final):**

The PA Supreme Court has an opportunity to correct this.

The Petition for Allowance of Appeal will ask: Can courts affirm void orders?

The answer will affect every trust in Pennsylvania.

#EstatePlanning #TrustAdministration
