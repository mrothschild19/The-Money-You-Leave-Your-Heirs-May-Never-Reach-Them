Pennsylvania Superior Court Affirms Trust Orders Without Jurisdiction; Ten Beneficiaries Denied Due Process

Appeal to PA Supreme Court Seeks to Void Orders Entered Without Required Parties

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — A petition for allowance of appeal filed with the Pennsylvania Supreme Court asks the state's highest court to address a fundamental breakdown in trust administration: what happens when courts enter orders affecting trust beneficiaries who were never given notice of the proceedings?

The case, In re Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust, centers on a Delaware County Orphans' Court proceeding that resulted in the appointment of a successor trustee and the freezing of trust assets—without ever notifying ten named beneficiaries who had vested interests in the trust.

"This isn't a technicality. Under Pennsylvania law, failure to join indispensable parties deprives the court of subject-matter jurisdiction. Every order entered without those parties is void from the start—they can never become valid, no matter how much time passes."

The Superior Court affirmed the Orphans' Court orders on April 1, 2026, despite the appellant presenting undisputed evidence that:

  • Ten beneficiaries with vested interests were never served with notice of the proceedings
  • Pennsylvania's Trust Code requires notice to all qualified beneficiaries before appointing a successor trustee
  • The proposed successor trustee who initiated the proceedings had no legal standing to do so under 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1
  • The orders resulted in the freezing of approximately $2 million in trust assets

The case highlights systemic vulnerabilities in Pennsylvania's probate system at a time when elder financial exploitation is reaching epidemic proportions. According to the U.S. Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), Americans lose an estimated $36.5 billion annually to elder financial exploitation—much of it through trust and estate schemes.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court has repeatedly held that jurisdictional defects cannot be waived and void orders can be challenged at any time. In Sprague v. Casey, 520 Pa. 38 (1988), the Court stated that "the validity of an order is... always subject to challenge" when the court lacks jurisdiction.

A motion for reargument is currently pending before the Superior Court, with a decision expected by June 8, 2026. The petition for allowance of appeal to the Supreme Court will be filed within 30 days of any denial.

About the Case

  • Case Number: 1409 EDA 2024 (Superior Court); 0444-2023-OC (Orphans' Court)
  • Trust: Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust
  • Amount at Issue: Approximately $2 million in trust assets
  • Beneficiaries Affected: 10 individuals with vested interests
