### PRESS RELEASE BODY:

**DOWNINGTOWN, PA** — A petition for allowance of appeal filed with the Pennsylvania Supreme Court asks the state's highest court to address a fundamental breakdown in trust administration: what happens when courts enter orders affecting trust beneficiaries who were never given notice of the proceedings?

The case, *In re Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust*, centers on a Delaware County Orphans' Court proceeding that resulted in the appointment of a successor trustee and the freezing of trust assets—without ever notifying ten named beneficiaries who had vested interests in the trust.

"This isn't a technicality," said Miriam Roth Mandel, a beneficiary and the pro se appellant. "Under Pennsylvania law, failure to join indispensable parties deprives the court of subject-matter jurisdiction. Every order entered without those parties is void from the start—they can never become valid, no matter how much time passes."

The Superior Court affirmed the Orphans' Court orders on April 1, 2026, despite the appellant presenting undisputed evidence that:

- Ten beneficiaries with vested interests were never served
- Pennsylvania's Trust Code requires notice to all qualified beneficiaries
- The proposed successor trustee who initiated proceedings had no standing to do so
- The orders resulted in the freezing of approximately $2 million in trust assets

The case has attracted attention because it highlights systemic vulnerabilities in Pennsylvania's probate system at a time when elder financial exploitation is reaching epidemic proportions. According to the U.S. Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), Americans lose an estimated $36.5 billion annually to elder financial exploitation—much of it through trust and estate schemes.

"Every year, Pennsylvania families lose millions to guardianship and trust exploitation," said [Expert Quote - see below]. "This case demonstrates how easily the system can be manipulated to exclude the very people the law is supposed to protect."

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court has repeatedly held that jurisdictional defects cannot be waived and void orders can be challenged at any time. In *Sprague v. Casey*, 520 Pa. 38 (1988), the Court stated that "the validity of an order is... always subject to challenge" when the court lacks jurisdiction.

A motion for reargument is currently pending before the Superior Court, with a decision expected by June 8, 2026. The petition for allowance of appeal to the Supreme Court will be filed within 30 days of any denial.

**KEY LEGAL ISSUES**

1. **Jurisdictional Defect**: Failure to join indispensable parties under Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b) deprives court of subject-matter jurisdiction
2. **Void Orders**: Under Pennsylvania Supreme Court precedent, orders entered without jurisdiction are void ab initio and can never become valid
3. **Due Process Violation**: Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank (U.S. Supreme Court) requires notice to all interested parties
4. **Standing**: The proposed trustee who initiated proceedings had no legal standing under 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1

## PART VIII: RISK MITIGATION

### Legal Considerations

1. **Defamation Avoidance**: All statements must be factually accurate and based on court documents
2. **Privacy Protection**: Redact sensitive personal information from public documents
3. **Court Rules**: Ensure media activities don't violate court rules or standing orders
4. **Privilege**: Statements about judicial proceedings are generally protected, but avoid personal attacks

### Strategic Considerations

1. **Timing**: Coordinate with court deadlines; don't release before filing
2. **Tone**: Professional and factual; avoid emotional or accusatory language
3. **Documentation**: Every claim must be supported by documentary evidence
4. **Credibility**: One inaccuracy undermines entire narrative

### Contingency Planning

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| Media ignores story | Pursue alternative outlets; build social media presence |
| Opposing counsel responds | Prepare factual rebuttal; stay focused on documents |
| Court delays | Maintain momentum with updates; pivot to systemic issues |
| Negative coverage | Respond with facts; don't engage in disputes |
