When Courts Fail the Elderly: The Guardianship and Conservatorship Abuse Epidemic

January 29, 2026 | Reading Time: 35-40 minutes

By The Numbers

1.5 million adults under guardianship in the U.S.
$273 billion in assets controlled
97% non-prosecution rate for elder financial abuse

Introduction: The Fiction That Became Reality

In the 2020 Netflix thriller "I Care A Lot," Rosamund Pike plays a ruthless legal guardian who systematically exploits elderly victims, isolating them from family, draining their assets, and using the court system as her weapon. Critics praised the film's dark satire. Victims' advocates called it a documentary.

"People think it's exaggerated. It's not. If anything, the real cases are worse because they're happening to real people, right now, with courts' full approval."

The numbers tell a chilling story: 1.5 million adults are under guardianship or conservatorship in the United States, controlling approximately $273 billion in assets. Yet oversight is minimal, abuse is rampant, and prosecution is virtually nonexistent.

From Wendy Williams—the beloved talk show host whose guardianship has been investigated as potential "elder abuse"—to disabled siblings in Philadelphia denied their inheritance for 51 months while court-appointed trustees extract fees, the pattern is consistent: vulnerable victims, fiduciary betrayal, court complicity, and zero accountability.

Part I: The Wendy Williams Case—When Fame Can't Protect You

The Disappearance of a Cultural Icon

Wendy Williams, 60, built a media empire on her unfiltered honesty and connection with fans. But in recent years, she has virtually disappeared from public view, isolated under a court-appointed guardianship that her family and advocates say constitutes elder abuse.

The Timeline of Control

  • 2022: Wells Fargo freezes Williams' accounts, claiming she is an "incapacitated person"
  • February 2022: Court appoints Sabrina Morrissey as Williams' guardian
  • 2023-2024: Williams isolated from family, friends, and public
  • February 2024: Lifetime documentary reveals Williams' diagnosis of primary progressive aphasia and frontotemporal dementia
  • 2024-2025: Family members publicly state they cannot contact Williams or know her location
  • January 2025: Investigation opened into potential "elder abuse" in Williams' guardianship

The Pattern: Isolation, Control, Exploitation

Williams' niece, Alex Finnie, told media outlets: "We don't know where she is. We can't talk to her. The guardian controls everything."

This is the classic guardianship abuse playbook:

  1. Isolate the victim from family and support systems
  2. Control all communication and access
  3. Drain assets through fees, expenses, and questionable transactions
  4. Use court orders to prevent family intervention
  5. Claim it's all "for their own good"

Sound familiar? It should. This same pattern appears in guardianship and conservatorship cases nationwide—including in trust and estate cases where court-appointed fiduciaries exploit their positions.

Part II: The Philadelphia Case—When Trustees Become Hijackers

The Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust

While Wendy Williams' fame brought media attention to her guardianship, most victims suffer in obscurity. Consider the case of two disabled siblings in Philadelphia whose inheritance has been held hostage for 51 months—not by strangers, but by attorneys who allegedly hijacked their family trust.

Case Details

Case No.: 15-25-2005, Court of Common Pleas of Philadelphia County, Orphans' Court Division

The Alleged Scheme

According to court filings, Edward L. Perkins of Gibson & Perkins, PC filed a petition claiming "is the Trustee" of the Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust 22 times under oath—despite allegedly never being legally appointed as trustee.

This foundational fraud allegedly gave Perkins control over an $800,000+ trust meant for two disabled beneficiaries: Elaina Rothenberg and her brother Lee Roth.

The Attorney-Trustee Dual Role: A License to Steal?

What makes this case particularly egregious is that both Perkins and his law partner, Walter J. Timby, III, claim to serve dual roles as both trustees AND legal counsel for the trust.

The perverse incentives are obvious:

  • More litigation = More billable hours
  • More delays = More fees extracted
  • More obstruction = Less accountability
  • All paid from the beneficiaries' own inheritance

According to court documents, Perkins and Timby have extracted over $50,000 in fees while denying the disabled beneficiaries access to their inheritance for 44+ months (now 51 months and counting).

The Obstruction Playbook

When beneficiary Elaina Rothenberg (proceeding pro se) attempted to hold the trustees accountable, court filings allege a systematic campaign of obstruction:

  1. False Statements About Discovery Law - On January 27, 2026, Timby allegedly wrote to Rothenberg claiming discovery was stayed—a statement contradicted by Pennsylvania law.
  2. Improper Blanket Protective Orders - Timby allegedly obtained a blanket protective order with only 12 hours notice.
  3. Evidence Suppression - Critical documents allegedly withheld from discovery.
  4. Complete Refusal to Cooperate - Despite formal discovery requests, no cooperation provided.

Part III: The National Epidemic—By The Numbers

The Government Accountability Office Investigation (2010)

In 2010, the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) investigated guardianship abuse and documented what it called "a real cottage industry" of exploitation:

Key GAO Findings

  • $5.4 million stolen from 158 victims in just 20 cases examined
  • Courts failed to screen guardians before appointment
  • Courts failed to oversee guardians after appointment
  • Victims were elderly or disabled with no one to advocate for them

The GAO concluded: "These cases illustrate the potential for guardians to exploit their wards and the failure of court systems to detect and prevent such abuse."

Sacramento County: A Case Study in Failure

In June 2025, a Sacramento County Grand Jury issued a damning report titled "No Equal Justice for Victims of Elder Financial Abuse."

Sacramento County Statistics (2019-2024)

3,987 confirmed elder abuse cases
99 prosecutions filed (2.5% rate)
97% of confirmed cases resulted in no prosecution

The Grand Jury found "systemic failures at every level of the criminal justice system" and concluded that elder financial abuse has become "de facto legal" in Sacramento County.

Conclusion: The System Is Working As Designed

The guardianship and conservatorship abuse epidemic is not a failure of the system—it is the system working as designed.

The System Serves:

  • Professional guardians who profit from controlling wards
  • Attorneys who bill estates for defending guardians
  • Courts that rely on professional guardians as repeat players
  • Prosecutors who avoid complex, resource-intensive cases

The System Does NOT Serve:

  • Vulnerable elderly and disabled victims
  • Families trying to protect loved ones
  • Beneficiaries denied their inheritance
  • The public interest in protecting the vulnerable
"I Care A Lot" wasn't fiction. The 2020 film depicted a ruthless guardian systematically exploiting elderly victims. Critics called it dark satire. It was actually a documentary.

What You Can Do

For Individuals

  1. Create advance directives - Specify who you trust before incapacity
  2. Designate power of attorney - Choose your own agent
  3. Document your wishes - Make your preferences clear
  4. Stay connected - Maintain family and social relationships
  5. Monitor finances - Keep track of your assets

For Families

  1. Stay involved - Regular contact with elderly/disabled relatives
  2. Document everything - Keep records of all interactions
  3. Question authority - Don't assume guardians act in good faith
  4. Contact media - Public pressure sometimes works
  5. Support reform - Advocate for legislative changes

For Society

  1. Demand prosecution - Contact District Attorneys
  2. Support legislation - Contact state representatives
  3. Require oversight - Demand court accountability
  4. Raise awareness - Share these stories
