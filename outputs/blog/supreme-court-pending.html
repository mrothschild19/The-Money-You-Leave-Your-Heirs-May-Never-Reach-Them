The Core Legal Question

At the heart of this case is a fundamental question about the rule of law:

Are court orders issued without jurisdiction void from the start, or must families accept them simply because a judge signed them?

Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure 2227(b) requires that indispensable parties be joined in any action affecting their interests. The failure to join indispensable parties is not a mere procedural technicality—it deprives the court of jurisdiction.

Orders issued without jurisdiction are void ab initio—void from the start. They have no legal effect and can be challenged at any time.

But getting courts to acknowledge that they lacked jurisdiction when they signed orders is another matter entirely.

The Catch-22

The Superior Court acknowledged that not all beneficiaries received notice. But rather than finding that the Orphans' Court lacked jurisdiction, the court held that the appellant "waived" the issue by not raising it sooner.

"How can you waive an objection to proceedings you were never told about? That's like saying you waived your right to attend a meeting you were never invited to."

This creates an impossible situation: beneficiaries who were never notified couldn't object to proceedings they didn't know about. Now, courts are saying they should have objected earlier. It's a legal circle that leaves families with no recourse.

What's at Stake

If the Pennsylvania Supreme Court declines to hear the case, or rules against the beneficiaries:

  • Court procedures can be exploited without consequence
  • The message goes out that due process doesn't matter once a judge signs
  • Every trust in Pennsylvania remains vulnerable to the same hijacking scheme

If the Court grants review and finds the orders void:

  • It would establish that jurisdiction matters
  • Every Pennsylvania family with a trust would gain a layer of protection
  • Courts would have to ensure all beneficiaries receive notice before appointing successor trustees

The Broader Implication

This isn't just about one trust. Every trust in Pennsylvania could be vulnerable to the same hijacking scheme. If courts won't enforce joinder requirements, then the rule of law means nothing—and trust predators have a roadmap.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court has an opportunity to protect families across the Commonwealth. We're asking them to take it.

What Happens Next

The Supreme Court will decide whether to grant allowance of appeal. If granted, the case will proceed to full briefing and argument. If denied, the Superior Court's decision stands.
