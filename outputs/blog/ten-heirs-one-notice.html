Ten Heirs, One Notice

April 25, 2026 Miriam Roth Mandel
victims family beneficiaries

How nine trust beneficiaries were kept in the dark while a stranger took control of their family's assets—with the court's blessing.

The Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust

Mitchell and Karlie Roth spent their lives building an estate to leave to their children and grandchildren. They did everything right: they consulted attorneys, created a trust, and named their beneficiaries. They believed their family was protected.

When they passed away, their trust named ten heirs—children and grandchildren who would inherit what their parents and grandparents had worked their entire lives to build.

The Petition

In 2023, a petition was filed in Delaware County Orphans' Court seeking appointment of a "proposed trustee" for the Roth family trust. The petitioner claimed the trust needed a successor trustee and nominated himself for the position.

According to the petition, only one party required notice: Linnea Leah Roth, one of the ten heirs.

The other nine heirs—including the actual trust beneficiaries—were not identified as parties requiring notice. They never received any notification that a stranger was petitioning to take control of their inheritance.

The Notice That Never Came

Think about that for a moment. Nine people who stood to inherit from their parents' trust had no idea that someone was asking a court to give him control of that trust.

They didn't know a petition had been filed. They didn't know a hearing was scheduled (if there even was one). They didn't have an opportunity to object, to appear, to ask questions, or to protect their interests.

By the time they learned what had happened, the court orders were already signed. A stranger had been appointed successor trustee. He now had legal control over their family's assets.

The Beneficiary Who Did Receive Notice

Linnea Leah Roth, the one heir who did receive notice, did not object to the petition. Why not? We can't speak for her. But it's worth noting that failing to object is not the same as consenting—and certainly not the same as consenting on behalf of nine other beneficiaries who never received notice.

One notice. One non-objection. And just like that, a stranger controlled the trust.

The Fundamental Problem

Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure 2227(b) requires that indispensable parties be joined in any action affecting their interests. The failure to join indispensable parties deprives the court of jurisdiction.

Trust beneficiaries are indispensable parties to a proceeding appointing a successor trustee. They have a direct financial interest in who controls the trust assets.

Yet under current practice, petitioners identify who they believe requires notice—and courts proceed based on those representations without independent verification.

"How can you object to proceedings you were never told about? That's like saying you waived your right to attend a meeting you were never invited to."

The Human Cost

Beyond the legal arguments, there's a human story here. Nine heirs discovered that their inheritance had been handed to a stranger without their knowledge. They've spent three years fighting through the court system to undo what should never have happened.

The stress. The expense. The frustration of a system that failed them at every turn. The realization that the court system meant to protect families became the weapon used against them.

This is what trust hijacking looks like in human terms.

What Should Have Happened

In a system that protected families:

  • All ten heirs would have received notice
  • All ten heirs would have had an opportunity to object
  • The court would have verified the petitioner's relationship to the trust
  • A hearing would have been held to determine if the appointment was appropriate
  • The court would have verified that the trust actually needed a successor trustee

None of that happened. And now we're fighting to make it right.

The Bigger Picture

If this can happen to the Roth family, it can happen to any family in Pennsylvania. Every trust is potentially vulnerable. Every inheritance is potentially at risk.

That's why we're telling this story. Not just for our family—but for every family that could be next.
