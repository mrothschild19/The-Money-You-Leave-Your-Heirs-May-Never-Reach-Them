How to Hijack a Trust in Pennsylvania

April 25, 2026 Miriam Roth Mandel
how-to orphans court legal loophole

The disturbingly simple process that allows strangers to seize control of family trusts through Orphans' Court—with no notice to all beneficiaries.

Warning: This is not a guide for perpetrators. It's an exposé of a vulnerability that needs to be closed. Understanding how trust hijacking works is the first step to preventing it.

Step 1: Identify a Target Trust

Trust documents are often filed with county courts and become public records. Pennsylvania's Unified Judicial System website (ujsportal.pacourts.us) allows anyone to search court dockets. With some research, a determined individual can identify trusts with assets worth targeting.

Factors that make a trust vulnerable:

  • Elderly settlors or trustees
  • Multiple beneficiaries in different locations
  • Family estrangements or blended families
  • Trust documents that don't specify clear succession

Step 2: File a Petition

Walk into the Orphans' Court division of the county Court of Common Pleas. File a petition requesting appointment as successor trustee. Claim to be a "proposed trustee" with an interest in the trust's administration.

Under current Pennsylvania practice, you don't need to prove:

  • That you're named in the trust document
  • That you have any relationship to the beneficiaries
  • That the trust is actually vacant (needs a new trustee)
  • That the current trustee is unable or unwilling to serve

Step 3: Minimal Notice

Here's the key vulnerability: you only need to serve notice on parties you identify as interested.

In our case, the petitioner identified only one of ten heirs as requiring notice. The other nine—including the actual trust beneficiaries—never received notice. The court proceeded based on the petitioner's representation of who was "interested."

By the time the other beneficiaries learned what had happened, the court orders were already signed.

Step 4: Get the Order

With no objections from parties who never received notice, the judge signs the order. Congratulations—you're now the successor trustee with legal authority to control the trust assets.

Judges rely on the representations in petitions. With no adversarial process and no one to object, there's no reason for the court to suspect anything is wrong.

Step 5: Control the Assets

As successor trustee, you now have authority to:

  • Access trust bank accounts
  • Sell trust property
  • Make distribution decisions
  • Hire professionals (attorneys, accountants) who bill the trust
  • Collect trustee fees

The beneficiaries who were never notified may not discover the takeover for months or years—until they try to access their inheritance.

Why This Is Legal

That's the most insidious part: all of this happens through valid court procedures. The predator doesn't forge documents. Doesn't commit fraud in the traditional sense. Simply uses a court system designed for efficiency, not protection against exploitation.

The orders are signed by a judge. The appointment is on the court record. It's "legal"—even if the court never had jurisdiction because indispensable parties weren't joined.

How to Close This Loophole

The fix is simple:

  1. Mandatory notice to all beneficiaries: Before any successor trustee appointment, require verified proof that all beneficiaries received actual notice.
  2. Petitioner qualification requirements: Require petitioners to demonstrate a legitimate connection to the trust.
  3. Hearings for contested appointments: Mandate adversarial proceedings when any beneficiary objects or when the petitioner isn't named in the trust.
  4. Court verification of vacancy: Require courts to independently verify that the trust actually needs a new trustee.

These reforms would close the loophole without disrupting legitimate trust administration.

What Happened to Us

In 2023, someone followed these exact steps and gained control of our family's trust. We've spent three years fighting through Delaware County Orphans' Court, Superior Court, and now the Pennsylvania Supreme Court to undo what should never have happened.

We're telling this story so other families don't have to go through what we've been through. And so legislators understand exactly how the system is being exploited.

This actually happened. It can happen to any Pennsylvania family. It needs to stop.
