The Money You Leave Your Children May Never Reach Them

How a Pennsylvania Lawyer Walked Into Orphans' Court and Walked Out With Control of a $2 Million Family Trust—Without Ever Notifying the Ten Heirs Named in It

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — You spend your life building your estate. You hire lawyers. You sign documents. You create a trust to protect your children and grandchildren. You assume that when you're gone, your money will go where you intended.

But what if it doesn't? What if someone can walk into a Pennsylvania courthouse, claim to be a "proposed trustee," and walk out with control of everything you saved—even if they're not a beneficiary, even if they have no legal relationship to your family, and even if the people you named in your trust never even know it happened?

THIS ACTUALLY HAPPENED. In Delaware County, Pennsylvania, an attorney nominated himself as successor trustee of a $2 million family trust, filed paperwork in Orphans' Court, and obtained control of trust assets—without ever notifying ten beneficiaries named in the trust document. By the time the beneficiaries learned what had happened, their inheritance was frozen and under the control of a stranger.

The case is In re Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust. Mitchell and Karlie Roth were Pennsylvania residents who created a trust for their family—two daughters and eight grandchildren. All ten were named beneficiaries with vested interests. Under the trust document, they were supposed to inherit.

But after the settlors passed away, an attorney named Edward L. Perkins filed a petition in Delaware County Orphans' Court seeking appointment as successor trustee. He was not a beneficiary. He had no family relationship to the trust. He was, essentially, a stranger who nominated himself.

And the court gave him control.

"My parents spent their lives building this trust for their family. They thought they were protecting us. They had no idea that someone could just walk into court, claim to be in charge, and freeze everything. There was no hearing we were invited to. No notice was sent. Just a court order appearing out of nowhere saying a stranger now controlled our inheritance."
— Miriam Roth Mandel, beneficiary and appellant

The numbers are staggering. This trust held approximately $2 million. But there's nothing stopping the same thing from happening to a $50,000 trust or a $50 million trust. The process is the same. The vulnerability is the same. And most families have no idea it exists.

WHAT HAPPENED

  • The Trust: Created by Mitchell and Karlie Roth for ten named beneficiaries
  • The Assets: Approximately $2 million
  • The "Proposed Trustee": An attorney who nominated himself—not a beneficiary, not family
  • Beneficiaries Notified: ONE of ten
  • The Result: A stranger walked out of court with control of the family's money

Pennsylvania law requires that all qualified beneficiaries receive notice before a successor trustee is appointed. 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(b) is explicit: "All qualified beneficiaries shall be given notice of the proceeding." But in this case, nine of ten beneficiaries—90% of the people the trust was created to protect—received nothing.

The court never asked why. The attorney never explained why. The proceedings went forward, and the orders were signed. By the time the beneficiaries found out, the trust was under new management—and their money was frozen.

This is not a hypothetical. It is not a cautionary tale. It happened in Pennsylvania courts, and under current law, it could happen to any family with a trust.

How to Hijack a Trust in Pennsylvania: A Step-by-Step Guide

No Beneficiary Verification. No Standing Requirement. No Notice to Heirs. How Pennsylvania's Orphans' Court System Creates an Open Door for Trust Predators

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — If you wanted to steal a family's trust, you'd have to break into their house, forge documents, and hope nobody noticed. Unless, that is, you're in Pennsylvania—in which case you just need to file some paperwork and wait for a judge to sign.

The Roth Trust case reveals a stunning vulnerability in Pennsylvania's trust administration system: there is no verification that the person petitioning for appointment as trustee has any right to be there. There is no requirement that beneficiaries confirm the petitioner's standing. There is, apparently, no check at all.

HERE'S HOW IT WORKS:

Step 1: File a Petition. Anyone can file a petition in Orphans' Court seeking appointment as successor trustee. You don't need to be a beneficiary. You don't need to be family. You don't need to show any legal relationship to the trust. You simply nominate yourself as "proposed trustee" and ask the court to approve.

Step 2: Provide Minimal Notice. Pennsylvania law technically requires notice to all qualified beneficiaries. But if you're the one filing the petition, you're also the one responsible for identifying who those beneficiaries are. There's no independent verification. If you "forget" nine of ten beneficiaries, nobody checks.

Step 3: Get the Order. If no one shows up to object—because no one was told about the hearing—the court issues an order appointing you trustee. You now have legal control over trust assets. You can freeze accounts, pay yourself fees, hire attorneys, and distribute (or not distribute) funds as you see fit.

Step 4: The Money is Yours to Control. Once appointed, the successor trustee has broad authority. Beneficiaries who weren't notified may not find out until they try to access funds—only to discover that a stranger is now in charge of their inheritance.

"We're not talking about a sophisticated heist. We're talking about paperwork. A lawyer filed documents saying he should be trustee. The court said okay. And just like that, a $2 million family trust was under the control of someone who wasn't a beneficiary, wasn't family, and had no legal standing to be there. The system didn't catch it. The system enabled it."
— Miriam Roth Mandel

THE STANDING PROBLEM

Under 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(a), only a "qualified beneficiary" may petition for appointment of a successor trustee. Edward L. Perkins was not a beneficiary. He had no standing. The court appointed him anyway.

Question: If standing requirements aren't enforced, what stops anyone from petitioning to take control of any trust?

This isn't about one bad actor. It's about a system designed to trust that the people appearing before it are following the rules. When they don't—when they "forget" to notify beneficiaries, when they nominate themselves despite lacking standing—there's no safety net. The court signs the order, and the money changes hands.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court has held that orders entered without proper parties are void—without legal effect. But that's little comfort to families who have to spend years in litigation to get that determination, while trust assets are drained by legal fees and the appointee remains in control.

The Roth Trust had $2 million. But the same process could hijack a $50,000 trust or a $50 million trust. The vulnerability doesn't discriminate by size.

Ten Heirs, One Notice: The Family Frozen Out of Their Own Inheritance

Mitchell and Karlie Roth Created a Trust for Their Two Daughters and Eight Grandchildren. Nine of Them Were Never Told a Stranger Had Taken Control.

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — Mitchell and Karlie Roth were Pennsylvania residents who worked hard, saved carefully, and created an irrevocable trust to provide for their family after they were gone. The trust document named ten beneficiaries: their two adult daughters, Miriam and Linnea, and eight grandchildren—David, Mitchell, Sara, Sean, Gracie, Alexander, Nora, and Charles.

Each beneficiary had a vested interest. Each was named in the trust. Each was supposed to be protected.

But when an attorney petitioned to become successor trustee of the Roth family trust, only one of those ten people received notice. The other nine—including adult children and grandchildren who would inherit from the trust—learned about the court proceedings only after orders had been entered transferring control to a stranger.

"My parents thought they had done everything right. They created a trust. They named us as beneficiaries. They assumed the law would protect us. They never imagined that someone could go to court, leave 90% of the family in the dark, and walk away with control of everything they had saved. But that's exactly what happened."
— Miriam Roth Mandel, daughter of the trust settlors

The ten beneficiaries span generations. Some are adults building their own lives. Some are young people just starting out. All of them had a legal interest in the trust that their grandparents created for their benefit.

Under Pennsylvania law, all of them should have received notice. 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(b) couldn't be clearer: "All qualified beneficiaries shall be given notice of the proceeding." Not one beneficiary. Not the ones who are convenient. All of them.

But notice wasn't given. And the court didn't ask why.

When Courts Rubber-Stamp Trust Hijackings: The Appellate Failure

Superior Court Affirmed Orders Entered Without Required Parties—Treating a Void Proceeding as if It Were Valid

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — When ten beneficiaries are excluded from a trust proceeding, you might expect an appellate court to notice. You might expect judges to ask why 90% of the affected parties weren't in the room. You might expect the system to correct its own errors.

You would be wrong.

On April 1, 2026, the Pennsylvania Superior Court affirmed the Orphans' Court orders appointing Edward L. Perkins as successor trustee—despite undisputed evidence that nine of ten beneficiaries had never been notified, and despite the fact that Perkins himself lacked standing under the very statute he invoked.

The affirmance came with no explanation of how a court can obtain jurisdiction without the required parties. No analysis of why standing requirements weren't enforced. No discussion of what happens to due process when the people most affected by a proceeding are excluded from it.

"The Superior Court treated this like a normal appeal from a normal order. But this wasn't normal. The trial court never had jurisdiction because required parties weren't before it. Under Pennsylvania Supreme Court precedent, orders entered without jurisdiction are void—they're legal nullities. You can't affirm something that doesn't exist. But that's exactly what the Superior Court did."
— Miriam Roth Mandel, pro se appellant

Pennsylvania law is clear on void orders. In Sprague v. Casey, 520 Pa. 38 (1988), the Supreme Court held that "the validity of an order is... always subject to challenge" when the court lacks jurisdiction. In McEvoy v. Quaker City Cab Co., 267 Pa. 527 (1920), the Court stated that a void order "may be attacked anywhere... by parties or strangers."

A void order, in other words, has no legal effect. It cannot be enforced. It cannot be appealed in the traditional sense—because there's nothing to appeal. It simply doesn't exist.

Yet the Superior Court affirmed the Orphans' Court orders as if they were valid. The beneficiaries' due process claims were effectively ignored. The jurisdictional defect was treated as a technicality rather than a fundamental flaw that renders the entire proceeding illegitimate.

THE LEGAL REALITY

  • Failure to join indispensable parties = no jurisdiction (Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b))
  • No jurisdiction = void order (Sprague v. Casey, 520 Pa. 38)
  • Void order = no legal effect, can be challenged anytime (McEvoy, 267 Pa. 527)
  • Yet Superior Court affirmed anyway

The question for the Pennsylvania Supreme Court: Can appellate courts affirm orders that are void on their face?

A Motion for Reargument is currently pending before the Superior Court, with a decision expected by June 8, 2026. A Petition for Allowance of Appeal to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court will follow any denial.

But the damage is already done. While the legal process plays out, a stranger remains in control of a family's inheritance. Trust assets are being used to pay attorneys to defend proceedings that never should have occurred. And the message to other potential trust hijackers is clear: the courts won't stop you.

If Pennsylvania's appellate courts won't enforce beneficiary notice requirements, who will?

$36 Billion Stolen Annually—And Much of It Happens Through "Legal" Court Proceedings

The Roth Trust Case Is Not an Isolated Incident. It's Part of an Epidemic of Elder Financial Exploitation Enabled by Courts Across America.

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — When we think of financial exploitation, we imagine scammers calling elderly victims, or family members stealing from a grandmother's purse. But the fastest-growing form of elder financial abuse doesn't look like a crime at all. It looks like a court proceeding.

Guardianships. Conservatorships. Trust administration. These are legal processes designed to protect vulnerable adults. But in the wrong hands, they become weapons—allowing predators to gain court-sanctioned control over assets while bypassing the very people those assets were meant to benefit.

$36.5 BILLION

Lost annually to elder financial exploitation in the United States (FinCEN, 2024)

The U.S. Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) reports that elder financial exploitation costs Americans $36.5 billion every year. The National Council on Aging estimates that only 1 in 44 cases is ever reported. The true scope of the problem is likely far larger.

And increasingly, the exploitation happens through legal channels. An adult child files for guardianship without telling siblings. A caretaker petitions to be appointed trustee without notifying the heirs. A lawyer drafts a new will that leaves everything to himself. All of it blessed by a judge. All of it technically "legal."

"People assume that if something goes through court, it must be legitimate. But courts don't verify the facts—they rely on the people appearing before them to tell the truth. When those people have an agenda, the whole system becomes a tool for exploitation. The Roth Trust case is what that looks like in practice."
— Miriam Roth Mandel

Pennsylvania has recognized the problem. In 2025, Attorney General Dave Sunday launched an Elder Exploitation Section dedicated to prosecuting financial crimes against seniors. Spotlight PA's "Unprotected" series documented systemic failures in the state's elder protection agencies. Governor Shapiro's administration has called for reforms.

But little attention has been paid to exploitation through probate courts themselves. The Roth Trust case shows that the courthouse can be just as dangerous as any scam—and far more difficult to fight.

EXPLOITATION THROUGH "LEGAL" MEANS

  • Guardianships: Can strip away all rights without the person's knowledge or consent
  • Trust proceedings: Can transfer control of assets without notifying beneficiaries
  • Powers of attorney: Can be abused with little oversight until it's too late
  • Will contests: Can drain estates while families fight over what's left

Common thread: All of these processes rely on courts trusting that the people appearing before them are following the rules. When they don't, there's often no safety net.

The Great Wealth Transfer is underway—an estimated $124 trillion will pass between generations over the coming decades. For predators, this represents an unprecedented opportunity. For families, it represents unprecedented risk.

The Roth Trust had $2 million. But the same mechanisms that allowed a stranger to take control of that trust could be deployed against any family trust in Pennsylvania, regardless of size. The courthouse door is open. The process is simple. And most families have no idea it's possible.

Closing the Courthouse Door to Trust Predators: Reforms Pennsylvania Needs Now

The Roth Trust Case Exposes Systemic Vulnerabilities. Here's What Must Change to Protect Pennsylvania Families

DOWNINGTOWN, PA — The Roth Trust case is not just a legal dispute. It's a warning. If Pennsylvania's system allowed a stranger to take control of a $2 million family trust without notifying the heirs, the same thing could happen to any family with a trust—no matter how carefully that trust was drafted, no matter how clear the beneficiaries' rights.

The legal battle continues. A Motion for Reargument is pending before the Superior Court. A Petition for Allowance of Appeal to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court will follow. But litigation is reactive—it fights over what already happened. To protect families going forward, Pennsylvania needs systemic reform.

HERE'S WHAT NEEDS TO CHANGE:

1. Mandatory Beneficiary Verification. Before appointing any successor trustee, courts must independently verify that all qualified beneficiaries have been identified and served. The current system allows petitioners to "forget" beneficiaries with no consequences. Courts should require an affidavit listing all beneficiaries and confirming that notice has been sent to each one.

2. Standing Must Be Enforced. Under 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(a), only a "qualified beneficiary" may petition for appointment of a successor trustee. Yet in the Roth Trust case, a non-beneficiary petitioned and was appointed. Courts must verify standing before acting—not assume it exists.

3. Automatic Investigation of Missing Beneficiaries. When a trust document identifies multiple beneficiaries but only one appears in court, that should trigger immediate inquiry. Why are the others absent? Were they notified? The absence of interested parties should be a red flag, not a convenience.

4. Real Penalties for Notice Violations. Attorneys who fail to notify required parties should face consequences—sanctions, fee forfeiture, or disciplinary action. Currently, there's no downside to "forgetting" a beneficiary. The worst case is that the court eventually catches it; the best case (for the petitioner) is that no one notices.

5. Attorney General Oversight of Probate Exploitation. The Elder Exploitation Section should have explicit authority to investigate trust and guardianship abuse occurring through court proceedings, not just traditional fraud. When courts are used as weapons, law enforcement needs tools to respond.

"My parents did everything right. They created a trust. They named their beneficiaries. They thought their family was protected. They never imagined that the weakest link in the chain was the court system itself. No family should have to go through what we've been through. The system needs to change."
— Miriam Roth Mandel

WHAT FAMILIES CAN DO NOW:

Until the system is fixed, families must be vigilant:

  • Know your rights. If you're a trust beneficiary, request copies of the trust document and understand your interest.
  • Monitor court dockets. Check ujsportal.pacourts.us regularly for any filings affecting your family's trust.
  • Stay in touch with fiduciaries. Don't assume everything is fine. Ask questions. Request accountings.
  • Act quickly if something's wrong. Contact an elder law attorney immediately if you suspect exploitation.
  • Report to authorities. PA Elder Abuse Hotline: 1-800-490-8505. AG Elder Complaint: Seniorcomplaint@attorneygeneral.gov

KEY CONTACTS FOR FAMILIES

  • PA Elder Abuse Hotline: 1-800-490-8505
  • PA Attorney General Elder Complaints: www.attorneygeneral.gov/submit-a-complaint/senior-complaint
  • PA SeniorLAW HelpLine: 1-877-727-7529
  • Court Docket Search: ujsportal.pacourts.us
  • PA Disciplinary Board (attorney complaints): padisciplinaryboard.org

The Great Wealth Transfer will see $124 trillion change hands over the coming decades. Pennsylvania has an opportunity to lead in protecting families—or to remain a state where trust predators can operate with impunity.

The Roth family is fighting to protect what their parents built. But they shouldn't have to fight alone. It's time for Pennsylvania to close the courthouse door to trust hijackers—before more families discover that the inheritance they were counting on has been frozen, drained, or stolen by a stranger with a court order.

Because if it can happen to the Roth family, it can happen to anyone.
