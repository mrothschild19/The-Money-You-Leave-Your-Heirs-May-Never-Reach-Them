I. PROCEDURAL HISTORY

This matter arises from proceedings in the Delaware County Orphans' Court, Case No. 0444-2023-OC, concerning the purported nullification of certain trusts and the freezing of trust assets. The procedural history of this matter is as follows:

On April 18, 2024, the Orphans' Court of Delaware County entered orders (the "Nullity Orders") declaring certain trusts to be null and void and ordering the freezing of trust assets. These orders were entered without jurisdiction, as indispensable parties whose rights would be directly affected by the orders were not joined to the proceeding.

Appellant filed a timely appeal to the Superior Court of Pennsylvania, No. 1409 EDA 2024. On April 1, 2026, the Superior Court entered an Order affirming the Orphans' Court's Nullity Orders.

On April 7, 2026, Appellant filed a Motion for Reargument in the Superior Court, challenging the Court's affirmance of the Nullity Orders. On April 15, 2026, the Superior Court denied the Motion for Reargument.

On April 15, 2026, Appellant filed a Motion to Vacate the April 18, 2024 Nullity Orders in the Delaware County Orphans' Court, on the ground that the Court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction to enter those orders due to the failure to join indispensable parties and other jurisdictional defects. That motion remains pending before the Orphans' Court as of the date of this Petition.

This Petition for Allowance of Appeal is timely filed within thirty (30) days of the Superior Court's denial of the Motion for Reargument, pursuant to Pa.R.A.P. 1113(a).

II. STATEMENT OF JURISDICTION

The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania has jurisdiction over this matter pursuant to 42 Pa.C.S. § 724(a), which provides that the Supreme Court may allow an appeal from any final order of the Superior Court. The Order appealed from is a final order disposing of all claims and all parties.

III. STATEMENT OF THE STANDARD OF REVIEW

The standard of review for an appeal from an order of the Superior Court affirming an Orphans' Court decision is de novo. In re Estate of piedmont, 941 A.2d 680 (Pa. 2007). The Supreme Court reviews the Orphans' Court's findings of fact for clear error and its legal conclusions de novo. In re Estate of Poland, 221 A.3d 608 (Pa. 2019).

Questions of subject-matter jurisdiction are questions of law reviewed de novo. Murray v. O'Dowd, 928 A.2d 947 (Pa. 2007). Whether a court had jurisdiction to enter an order is a jurisdictional question that may be raised at any time, including for the first time on appeal. Vann v. Vann, 589 Pa. 459 (2006).

IV. SUMMARY OF THE ARGUMENT

The Superior Court committed reversible error by affirming the Orphans' Court's Nullity Orders, which were entered without subject-matter jurisdiction and which effectuated an unconstitutional taking of property without due process of law. Specifically:

First, the Orphans' Court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction to enter the Nullity Orders because indispensable parties were not joined to the proceeding. The trustee of the trusts at issue, whose rights and duties were directly affected by the Nullity Orders, was not a party to the proceeding. Under Pennsylvania law and fundamental principles of due process, a court cannot adjudicate the rights of absent indispensable parties. Orders entered without jurisdiction are void ab initio and may be challenged at any time.

Second, the Orphans' Court's Nullity Orders were based on the participation of a "proposed" trustee who had no legal status, standing, or authority to represent the trusts or their beneficiaries. The Superior Court erred by affirming orders that rely on the participation of a party with no standing to appear.

Third, the freezing of trust assets under the Nullity Orders constitutes an unconstitutional taking of property without due process of law in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article I, Section 10 of the Pennsylvania Constitution. Property cannot be seized or frozen pursuant to orders that are void for lack of jurisdiction.

Fourth, Appellant has a pending motion before the Orphans' Court to vacate the Nullity Orders on jurisdictional grounds. The Superior Court's affirmance of orders that may be vacated by the issuing court creates an untenable procedural posture that this Court should resolve.

V. ARGUMENT
A. The Orphans' Court Lacked Subject-Matter Jurisdiction to Enter the Nullity Orders Due to Failure to Join Indispensable Parties.

The Orphans' Court of Delaware County lacked subject-matter jurisdiction to enter the April 18, 2024 Nullity Orders because indispensable parties whose rights would be directly affected by those orders were not joined to the proceeding. Under Pennsylvania law, the failure to join indispensable parties deprives a court of subject-matter jurisdiction and renders any orders entered in that party's absence void ab initio.

The trustee of the trusts at issue—the very person whose rights and duties were most directly affected by the Nullity Orders—was not a party to the Orphans' Court proceeding. Instead, the proceeding involved a "proposed" trustee who had no legal status, no standing, and no authority to represent the trusts or their beneficiaries. A person who has been "proposed" as trustee but never appointed has no legal existence as a fiduciary and cannot bind the trust or appear in court on its behalf.

Pennsylvania Rule of Civil Procedure 1027(b)(1) provides that if a person who should be joined as a party cannot be joined, the court must determine whether the action should proceed in that person's absence. If the absent party is indispensable—that is, if the party's interests cannot be adequately represented by existing parties and the party's absence would impair the court's ability to render complete relief—the court cannot proceed. Murray v. O'Dowd, 928 A.2d 947 (Pa. 2007).

In this case, the trustee was indispensable to the proceeding. The Nullity Orders directly affected the trustee's rights, duties, and authority over trust property. Without the trustee's participation, the Orphans' Court could not adjudicate the validity of the trusts in a manner that would bind the trustee or the trust property. The failure to join the trustee deprived the Court of subject-matter jurisdiction.

Orders entered without jurisdiction are void ab initio and may be challenged at any time, including for the first time on appeal. Vann v. Vann, 589 Pa. 459 (2006); In re R.W., 110 A.3d 1076 (Pa. Super. 2015). A void order is a legal nullity—it confers no rights and imposes no obligations. The Superior Court cannot "affirm" a void order; such an order simply has no legal existence to affirm.

The Superior Court erred by affirming the Nullity Orders without addressing the fundamental jurisdictional defect that deprives those orders of any legal force. This Court should grant the Petition for Allowance of Appeal to address this critical question of first impression: whether a Superior Court may affirm orders that are void for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction.

B. The "Proposed" Trustee Had No Legal Status or Standing to Appear in the Orphans' Court Proceeding.

The Orphans' Court proceeding involved a "proposed" trustee—a person who had been nominated or suggested as trustee but who had never been appointed, qualified, or vested with any legal authority as a fiduciary. Under Pennsylvania trust law, a person does not become a trustee until they have accepted the trusteeship in accordance with the terms of the trust and applicable law. 20 Pa.C.S. § 7721. A "proposed" trustee has no legal existence as a fiduciary and has no standing to appear in court on behalf of the trust.

The participation of a "proposed" trustee in the Orphans' Court proceeding could not cure the jurisdictional defect caused by the absence of the actual trustee. A person with no legal status cannot bind the trust, cannot represent the beneficiaries' interests, and cannot confer jurisdiction on the court by voluntary appearance. The Superior Court erred by treating the "proposed" trustee's participation as a substitute for the joinder of the actual trustee.

This Court should grant the Petition for Allowance of Appeal to clarify the standards governing standing in trust litigation and to protect trusts from being bound by proceedings in which no proper fiduciary participated.

C. The Freezing of Trust Assets Constitutes an Unconstitutional Taking Without Due Process of Law.

The Nullity Orders directed the freezing of trust assets, effectively depriving Appellant and the beneficiaries of their property without due process of law. The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1. Article I, Section 10 of the Pennsylvania Constitution provides comparable protection.

Due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before a person is deprived of property. Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co., 339 U.S. 306 (1950). The freezing of trust assets pursuant to orders that are void for lack of jurisdiction cannot satisfy due process requirements. A void order cannot provide a lawful basis for the deprivation of property.

Moreover, the freezing of assets in this case was not accompanied by the procedural safeguards required by due process. There was no finding that the assets were at risk of dissipation. There was no opportunity for the true trustee to be heard on the question of whether the assets should be frozen. There was no provision for the prompt post-deprivation hearing required by Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).

The Superior Court erred by affirming orders that effectuate an unconstitutional taking of property. This Court should grant the Petition for Allowance of Appeal to address the constitutional dimension of this case and to protect the property rights of trust beneficiaries.

D. The Pending Motion to Vacate in the Orphans' Court Underscores the Jurisdictional Defect.

On April 15, 2026, Appellant filed a Motion to Vacate the Nullity Orders in the Delaware County Orphans' Court on the ground that the Court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction to enter those orders. That motion is currently pending. If the Orphans' Court grants the motion and vacates the Nullity Orders, the Superior Court's affirmance will be revealed as having affirmed orders that are void and without legal effect.

The pendency of this motion underscores the fundamental jurisdictional defect in this case. The Orphans' Court itself is being asked to recognize that it lacked jurisdiction to enter the Nullity Orders. A court has inherent power to recognize and correct its own jurisdictional defects at any time. Vann v. Vann, 589 Pa. 459 (2006). If the Orphans' Court grants the motion, this Court will have the opportunity to address the consequences of the Superior Court's affirmance of void orders.

This Court should grant the Petition for Allowance of Appeal to resolve the tension between the Superior Court's affirmance and the pending challenge to the Orphans' Court's jurisdiction. The orderly administration of justice requires that this Court provide guidance on whether the Superior Court may affirm orders that the issuing court may vacate as void.
