Appellant Miriam Roth Mandel, proceeding pro se, respectfully moves this Court to confirm that remittal of the record is stayed by operation of Pa.R.A.P. 2572(b), and to enter an order expressly staying remittal pending the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania's disposition of Appellant's forthcoming Petition for Allowance of Appeal. In support thereof, Appellant states as follows:

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 1, 2026, this Court entered a Memorandum Opinion affirming the April 18, 2024 Order of the Orphans' Court Division of the Delaware County Court of Common Pleas (Case No. 0444-2023-OC). Appellant timely filed an Application for Reargument, which this Court denied on April 15, 2026.

Appellant intends to file a Petition for Allowance of Appeal in the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania pursuant to Pa.R.A.P. 1113. The Petition for Allowance of Appeal is due on or before May 15, 2026 — thirty days after the April 15, 2026 denial of the Application for Reargument.

The Superior Court docket reflects that the record is scheduled for remittal to the Orphans' Court on or about May 1, 2026.

II. REMITTAL IS AUTOMATICALLY STAYED BY OPERATION OF RULE 2572(b)

Pennsylvania Rule of Appellate Procedure 2572(b) provides in pertinent part:

"Remand is stayed until disposition of: (1) an application for reargument; (2) any other application affecting the order; or (3) a petition for allowance of appeal from the order. The court possessed of the record shall remand 30 days after either the entry of a final order or the disposition of all post-decision applications, whichever is later."

The plain text of Rule 2572(b)(3) is dispositive: remittal is automatically stayed — by operation of rule, without any separate court order — upon the filing of a petition for allowance of appeal. The stay arises at the moment of filing and continues until the Supreme Court disposes of the petition.

Appellant files this Motion to ensure that this Court's Prothonotary is formally on notice that a Petition for Allowance of Appeal will be filed in this matter on or before May 15, 2026, and to obtain an express order confirming the stay so that the record is not inadvertently transmitted to the Orphans' Court during the pendency of the Petition for Allowance of Appeal.

III. IN THE ALTERNATIVE, A DISCRETIONARY STAY IS WARRANTED

To the extent this Court concludes that a discretionary stay under Pa.R.A.P. 1702(b) is required in addition to the automatic operation of Rule 2572(b), Appellant separately requests such relief. Rule 1702(b) provides that when a party intends to pursue discretionary appellate review, the party may seek a stay ancillary to the appeal prior to filing the petition for allowance of appeal.

The four-factor test for a stay pending appeal — derived from Pennsylvania Public Utility Commission v. Process Gas Consumers Group, 467 A.2d 805 (Pa. 1983) — is readily satisfied here.

Likelihood of success on the merits. The Petition for Allowance of Appeal presents three independent grounds that warrant allocatur: (1) the Orphans' Court lacked subject matter jurisdiction because indispensable parties — all trust beneficiaries — were never joined, rendering all orders void ab initio under 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1(b) and binding Pennsylvania Supreme Court precedent including Tigue v. Basalyga, 451 Pa. 436, 304 A.2d 119 (1973), Columbia Gas Transmission Corp. v. Diamond Fuel Co., 464 Pa. 377, 346 A.2d 788 (1975), and Sprague v. Casey, 520 Pa. 38, 550 A.2d 184 (1988); (2) the foundational petition named a non-juridical entity as petitioner, an independent jurisdictional defect; and (3) the proceedings were initiated by a legal stranger who lacked standing to petition for trustee appointment. Each ground independently deprived the Orphans' Court of subject matter jurisdiction and renders the resulting orders void without regard to their merits.

The Petition additionally presents a conflict-of-decisions basis for allocatur under Pa.R.A.P. 1114. This Court's April 1, 2026 affirmance is irreconcilable with In re Trust Under Agreement of Edward Winslow Taylor, 164 A.3d 1147 (Pa. 2017), and with this Court's own recent published decision in In re Estate of Dorothy A. Anderson, 2024 PA Super 117 (Pa. Super. June 5, 2024), which applied the indispensable party doctrine in a trust proceeding from the same Orphans' Court and reached a result consistent with the Supreme Court's teaching in Taylor.

Irreparable harm. If the record is remitted to the Orphans' Court before the Supreme Court acts on the Petition for Allowance of Appeal, the Orphans' Court may treat the remittal as a signal to proceed with enforcement of the affirmed orders — including orders effectuating a trustee replacement and the disposition of approximately $756,000 in trust assets. Trust assets, once distributed or transferred pursuant to a void order, may be exceedingly difficult or impossible to recover. Lee Benson Roth, the profoundly disabled primary beneficiary of the Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable 2002 Trust, has been denied access to funds created for his medical care for more than fifty-two months. Any further dissipation or encumbrance of those assets during the pendency of a meritorious allocatur petition would constitute irreparable harm.

Harm to other parties. Respondents cannot credibly claim harm from a stay of remittal lasting only until the Supreme Court acts on the Petition for Allowance of Appeal. The PAA will be filed and decided within the normal course of Supreme Court practice. No time-sensitive rights of Respondents depend on immediate remittal of the record.

Public interest. The questions presented in the Petition for Allowance of Appeal — whether indispensable parties must be joined in Orphans' Court trust proceedings, whether a non-juridical entity has standing to initiate such proceedings, and whether a legal stranger to a trust may petition for trustee appointment — are questions of statewide significance that affect every trust proceeding in Pennsylvania. Staying remittal to permit the Supreme Court to address these questions serves the public interest in the orderly development of Pennsylvania law.
