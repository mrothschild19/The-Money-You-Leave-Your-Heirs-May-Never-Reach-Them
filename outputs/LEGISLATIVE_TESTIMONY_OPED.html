I. The Problem: A "Trust Hijacking" Vulnerability

In Pennsylvania, a stranger can walk into Orphans' Court, file a petition claiming to be a "proposed trustee," and walk out with control of a family's trust assets. The process requires no hearing. No notice to all beneficiaries. No verification that the trust is actually vacant. No determination that the petitioner has any legitimate relationship to the trust or its beneficiaries.

Just a judge's signature—and suddenly, your family's inheritance belongs to someone else.

This isn't hypothetical. This actually happened.

The vulnerability exists at the intersection of three procedural gaps:

  1. The "Proposed Trustee" Loophole: Pennsylvania law allows anyone to petition for appointment as a successor trustee, but does not require verification of the petitioner's relationship to the trust or its beneficiaries.
  2. The Notice Gap: While Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b) requires joinder of all indispensable parties, Orphans' Court procedures do not require verified notice to all beneficiaries before appointing a successor trustee.
  3. The "Rubber Stamp" Problem: Judges, relying on the representations of petitioners and the absence of objections from parties who never received notice, sign orders without adversarial proceedings.

The Result:

Trust predators can exploit these gaps to gain control of family trust assets through "legal" court proceedings—proceedings that may be void from the start due to lack of jurisdiction, but that families must spend years and thousands of dollars to challenge.

II. The Case That Exposed It

Mitchell E. and Karlie M. Roth Irrevocable Trust

Docket: Delaware County Orphans' Court No. 0444-2023-OC; Superior Court No. 1409 EDA 2024

In 2023, a petition was filed in Delaware County Orphans' Court seeking appointment of a "proposed trustee" for the Roth family trust. The court issued orders appointing the petitioner as successor trustee. The problem:

  • Ten heirs. Only one received notice.
  • Nine beneficiaries—including the actual trust beneficiaries—were never notified.
  • No hearing was held to determine whether the trust was vacant or whether the petitioner had any legitimate claim.
  • No verification of the petitioner's relationship to the trust or its beneficiaries.

The beneficiary who did receive notice—Linnea Leah Roth, one of ten heirs—did not object. The other nine, kept in the dark, had no opportunity to object.

By the time the other beneficiaries learned what had happened, the orders were signed. A stranger now controlled their family's trust assets.

The legal challenge is now pending before the Pennsylvania Supreme Court on Petition for Allowance of Appeal. The core argument: orders issued without jurisdiction—specifically, without joinder of indispensable parties as required by Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b)—are void ab initio, meaning void from the start.

But here's the problem: even if the Supreme Court agrees, the family has spent years and significant resources fighting to undo what should never have happened in the first place. And other families across Pennsylvania are facing the same battle right now.

III. The $36 Billion Context

Elder Financial Exploitation: A National Crisis

According to FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network), elder financial exploitation is now a $36.5 billion annual problem in the United States. And that's only what's reported—the true figure is likely much higher.

The Pennsylvania Office of Attorney General launched its Elder Exploitation Section in 2025, recognizing that the Commonwealth's aging population makes this a growing crisis.

What makes trust hijacking particularly insidious is that it operates through the legal system. Predators don't need to hack bank accounts or forge signatures. They simply file petitions in Orphans' Court, obtain court orders, and walk away with legal authority to control family assets.

This is legalized theft—exploitation facilitated by the very courts that should be protecting families.

IV. The Legal Analysis: Why Current Law Fails

The vulnerability exists because Pennsylvania's trust administration procedures were designed for efficiency, not protection against exploitation. The underlying statutes—20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1 (successor trustee appointment) and related provisions—assume that petitioners are acting in good faith, that judges have sufficient information to evaluate petitions, and that interested parties will object if they have concerns.

These assumptions fail when:

  • Petitioners have no relationship to the trust or its beneficiaries and are seeking to exploit the system
  • Judges rely on petitioner representations without independent verification
  • Interested parties never receive notice and therefore cannot object
"The fundamental principle of due process is notice and an opportunity to be heard. When courts issue orders affecting property rights without ensuring that all interested parties have received actual notice, they risk depriving citizens of property without due process of law—regardless of whether the procedural rules technically permit it."

The joinder requirement in Pa.R.C.P. 2227(b) mandates that indispensable parties be joined in litigation affecting their interests. The failure to join indispensable parties is not a mere procedural defect—it deprives the court of jurisdiction. Orders issued without jurisdiction are void ab initio.

But the procedural gaps in current practice mean that beneficiaries often don't learn about trust hijacking until it's too late—until a stranger has already been appointed and has begun controlling the trust assets.

V. Proposed Reforms: Five Common-Sense Solutions

Legislative Recommendations

  1. Mandatory Verified Notice to All Beneficiaries
    Amend 20 Pa.C.S. § 7710.1 to require verified proof of service on all current beneficiaries before a court may appoint a successor trustee. No appointment should occur without documented proof that every beneficiary received actual notice and an opportunity to object.
  2. Petitioner Qualification Requirements
    Require petitioners for successor trustee appointment to demonstrate a legitimate connection to the trust or its beneficiaries—such as being named in the trust instrument, being a current beneficiary, or having a documented relationship with the settlor or beneficiaries. Strangers with no connection to the trust should not be able to self-nominate.
  3. Hearing Requirement for Contested or Unverified Appointments
    Mandate an adversarial hearing before a successor trustee is appointed in any case where: (a) any beneficiary objects; (b) the petitioner is not named in the trust instrument; or (c) the court cannot verify that all beneficiaries have received actual notice.
  4. Court Verification of Trust Vacancy
    Before appointing any successor trustee, require courts to make an independent determination that the trust is actually vacant and that the current trustee has resigned, been removed, or is unable to serve. This would prevent "trust hijacking" of trusts that have no vacancy.
  5. Enhanced Disclosure and Bond Requirements
    Require successor trustees appointed through court petitions to provide detailed financial disclosures to all beneficiaries and post a bond sufficient to protect the trust assets. This creates accountability and deters exploitation.

VI. Conclusion: A Call to Action

The Question Before This Committee

Is it acceptable in Pennsylvania for a stranger to walk into a courthouse and walk out with control of a family's trust assets—without notice to all beneficiaries, without a hearing, without verification?

The answer must be no.

The reforms I propose are not radical. They do not undermine legitimate trust administration. They simply require what families expect and what due process demands: notice, verification, and accountability.

Every day this loophole remains open, another Pennsylvania family is at risk. Trust predators are counting on inaction. They are counting on the complexity of probate procedure to shield their exploitation from public scrutiny.

Close the loophole. Protect Pennsylvania families. Act now, before the next family learns—too late—that the money they left their children will never reach them.
